Prevalence

Prevalence indicates

what proportion of the population is affected by the disease at specific time

Prevalence is calculated as:

\[\frac{\text{Number of Current Cases}}{\text{Total Population at a Specific Time}}\]

Prevalence type

  • Point prevalence: “right know”, “snaptshot”. Single point in time.

    Describe the current situation or to compare to previous situations (track development over time)


  • Period prevalence: “within 6 months”. Single time period. 
    Period prevalence:
    - Persons get sick during the period of ob.
    - Persons were already ill before (die in the period)

Properties and limitations

  • Important measure of the ‘Burden of Disease’
  • planning in the health system (in combination with demographic data projections possible)
  • In pharmacoepidemiology, e.g. as a measure of drug use
  • No recording of the time of onset of illness
  • Duration of illness does not go with (no information on duration)
  • No risk measure assessable, prevalence for atiological research (disease development, risk factors) unsuitable
  • Paradoxical situation: Since the prevalence reflects both the incidence rate and the probability of survival with a disease, prevalence studies provide information on factors influencing the probability of survival with a disease as well as on causes of the disease
  • Downsides of prevalence: mixes up information on (1) Frequency of new events and (2) disease duration
  • Does not capture dynamic populations (cure, death, migration…)

Incidence

Incidence

Incidence cont.

Cumulative Incidence

\(\begin{aligned} CI = \frac{\text{Number of New Cases}}{\text{Total Population at Risk}} \end{aligned}\)

  • Proportion or risk
  • No units
  • Assumes all individuals are followed for the same period
  • Probability of developing disease over a period

Incidence Rate

\(\begin{aligned} IR = \frac{\text{Number of New Cases}}{\text{Total Person-Time at Risk}} \end{aligned}\)

  • Rate
  • Units of time (e.g., per person-year)
  • Time that are a persons contributes (risk period)
  • Accounts for varying follow-up times
  • Speed at which new cases occur

Risk period, closed and open population


Incidence in dependence on the population:

  • Fixed/closed population: common beginning/end (exit only by death) of the observation (incidence measure: cumulative incidence)
  • Dynamic (open) population: entry (e.g. birth, immigration, ageing) and exit (e.g. death, emigration, ageing) possible at any time (incidence measure: incidence rate)

Steady-state: the number of people entering and exiting the population is balanced (also with regard to age, gender and other risk factors

Another measure that is very similar to the incidence rate and that is specifically used in survival analysis is the hazard rate. For all practical purposes, hazards can be thought of as incidence rates and thus the HR can be roughly interpreted as the incidence rate ratio (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3653612/). This means that the hazard rate can be thought of as the incidence rate of death or this other event (doi: 10.1093/ndt/gfw465)

Age standardisation

Age standardisation (arculatic matching of the age distribution of two groups):

  • Possible direct comparison of measures in 2 or more groups (age-specific measures are better comparable)
  • Calculation of a weighted average per group as a simple method of avoiding distortions by confounding (if age is associated with both exposure and outcome)
  • Standard can be decisive for conclusion: must be explained

Absolute differences in measures

Describes the difference between the probability that an event/outcome (e.g. an ADR/disease) occurs or not:

Definition: Absolute measures express the actual difference in risk or incidence between two groups, typically an exposed group and an unexposed group. Common examples include risk difference and attributable risk.


  • Prevalence difference (PD)
  • Risk difference (RD): Indicates the absolute difference between two risks \[RD= \text{Risk}_{\text{Exposed}} - \text{Risk}_{\text{Unexposed}}\]

    No Unit

  • Incidence rate difference (IRD) \[IRD= \text{IR}_{\text{Exposed}} - \text{IR}_{\text{Unexposed}}\]

    Unit: per person-years (such as incidence rate)

Characteristics AD:

Provides a straightforward interpretation of the actual impact of an exposure. Useful for public health decision-making as it indicates the actual number of cases that can be attributed to a risk factor. ffff

Relative Measures

Definition: Relative measures express the ratio of risks between two groups, such as relative risk (RR) or odds ratios (OR). They indicate how many times more (or less) likely an event is to occur in one group compared to another.

Characteristics: Summarizes information into a single value, making it easier to communicate findings. Can obscure the actual risk when absolute risks are low, leading to potential overestimation of treatment effects or risks associated with exposures.

Pros and cons

Pros and Cons Pros of Absolute Measures - Clarity: Offers clear information about the actual risk difference, which is often more relevant for patients and policymakers. - Relevance: More directly applicable to individual decision-making, as they reflect real-world implications of health interventions. - Public Health Impact: Helps in assessing the burden of disease and planning interventions by showing how many cases could potentially be prevented.

Cons of Absolute Measures - Limited Context: May not provide a complete picture if used alone, as they do not account for the relative context of risks across populations. - Variability: The interpretation can vary significantly based on population characteristics, which may lead to different conclusions in subgroup analyses.

Pros of Relative Measures - Comparative Insight: Facilitates comparisons across different studies and populations by standardizing risk assessments. - Highlighting Associations: Effective in demonstrating the strength of associations between exposures and outcomes, particularly in clinical trials.

Cons of Relative Measures - Potential Misleading Nature: Can exaggerate perceived benefits or risks, especially when absolute risks are low; for instance, a treatment might reduce mortality by 50%, but if the baseline risk is very low, the actual benefit may be minimal. - Overemphasis on Ratios: May lead to neglecting important absolute differences that could inform better health decisions.

Conclusion In epidemiology, both absolute and relative measures play crucial roles in understanding health outcomes. While absolute measures provide clarity on actual risks and their public health implications, relative measures offer valuable insights into comparative risks. The choice between these measures should depend on the context of the study and the information needs of stakeholders involved in health decision-making.
